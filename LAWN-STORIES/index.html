About This Project

Lawn Stories untangles the collective and personal histories of the American front lawn. Fictionalized accounts reveal the peculiar and sometimes baffling tales of personal struggles, triumphs, battles, and camaraderies between nature and neighbor.

It was created as a thesis project by Ashley Bock at the University of Michigan. You can check out the process of creating this site here.

Endless thanks to Franc Nunoo-Quarcoo, Sophia Brueckner, and Brandon Keelan for advising this project. To Karli Johnson, Lian Wardrop, Sam Bertin for being the best studio square. And to Ethan Krajewski, for patience, support, and everything else.

“

I’ll wait it out a few days. Then maybe I’ll get around to it.

Gregg Bronn
2010


Gregg Bronn, 54, hasn’t mowed his backyard in days, but says it’s nothing crazy. “It’s my property,” he claims. “It doesn’t matter.” That was before he saw an envelope taped to his front door. It read: “To Neighbor: CUT YOUR GRASS. You are a complete disgrace to this neighborhood. We will call the association every day until you cut it.”It was signed by several of Bronn’s neighbors. Bronn says that, despite the note, he will not be cutting his grass today. “I don’t want them to think they’ve bossed me around. I’ll wait it out a few days. Then maybe I’ll get around to it,” Bronn says.

“

He’s just such
a lawnaholic.

Jenny Field
2012

Jenny Field, 38, was outraged when she arrived home from vacation to see her neighbor had mowed her lawn while she was gone. “I was growing it out to prevent dust,”she stated. “We’re inches below our normal rainfall.” Field did not tell her neighbor the reasoning for her long grass, but she still claims this felt more like an attack than a misunderstanding. Her neighbor did not ask permission, though the two have shared lawn-care duties in the past. However, this time, Field “didn’t even feel like thanking him considering he just messed my lawn up. He’s just a lawnaholic.”

“

If he keeps doing it, I might get the police involved.

Ben Goodson
2015

Ben Goodson, 37, works nearly sixty hours a week and doesn’t mow his lawn that often. Next door, his neighbor’s lawn is kept consistently pristine and trimmed. There wasn’t a problem between the two until Goodson noticed that his neighbor had mowed over the property line onto Goodson’s lawn. He assumed it was a mistake. But then it happened again. And again. His neighbor kept over-mowing by few feet—or as Goodson put it, “trespassing!” He feels that the whole situation “is just so rude—possibly even vandalism!” In the meantime, Goodson went out and bought a “no trespassing” sign to post on the property line. He casually adds, “if he keeps doing it, I might get the police involved. I don’t know, we’ll see.”

“

Those poor children.

Molly & Jimmy Levinson
1956


Jimmy Levinson, 7, loves when the “fog truck” comes down the road. His mother, Molly, 28, claims that all the neighborhood kids hop on their bikes as fast as they can to chase it down the street. “They just get so excited!”says Molly. “We eat supper early on the days it comes.” Jimmy says the fog truck is a highlight of his week. He loves watching the “big clouds” come out the back—in fact, the sound of the motor alone is enough to get him running outside. “I’m glad the city is doing something to protect these children from those mosquitoes and their diseases. You hear terrible stories on the news. Those poor children.”

“

Curb appeal can make or break
a sale.

Susan McFoster
2015

Susan McFoster, 43, is an experienced realtor. She thinks that homeowners often underestimate the power of the front yard. “Curb appeal can make or break a sale you know,” she claims. And she’s seen how it can go wrong: neighbors with lawn ornament collections, broken–down cars, tacky shrines, trash–piles, dead grass, old couches, gopher problems. She has some advice for folks trying to sell their house next to an eyesore: “If they’re particularly stubborn about fixing it up, see if they’re breaking any codes from the neighborhood association or city and then file a complaint there,” she says. “Otherwise, you could offer to help or pay for a service yourself. Fences or tall shrubs always work nicely, too,” she says, winking.

No single feature of a suburban residential community contributes as much to the charm and beauty of the individual home and locality as a well-kept lawn.

Abraham Levitt
Founder of Levitt & Sons

“

I think she
talks to them.

Jessica Lee
2017

Jessica Lee, 32, tells all her friends about the “creepy” lady that lives next door. Lee’s obsession with her neighbor’s strange behavior has become a running joke with her friends (one even gifted Lee a pair of binoculars, which she now keeps by her front window). She points toward the house across the street—a dark blue bungalow littered with lawn ornaments. “You see that plastic deer by the door? Yeah she’ll come outside and carry it around while she ‘visits’ the others. I think she talks to them.” Several plastic gnomes, geese, deer, and other woodland creatures populate the front yard. “She rotates them three times a week. On the solstice, she puts them in a circle.”

“

I see much more wildlife in my yard these days.

Allison Bush
2012

Allison Bush, 28, hasn’t mowed her lawn in three months. She says her township is starting to take notice. They sent a written warning informing her that her yard had become a “nuisance” and if she didn’t cut it, they would hire someone and bring law enforcement with them. Bush claims that her lawn is better off. “It’s a real ecosystem now. I see much more wildlife in my yard these days. Lots of bugs, snakes, birds, toads.” Bush claims that habitat loss isn’t only caused by industry and agriculture—“it’s happening in our backyards. We’re part of it, too.” She’s ready to fight the township on the rules about lawns. “If I don’t want to pollute and destroy my yard, I shouldn’t be forced to. The real problems are the mowed lawns and the laws that enforce them.”

“

He can't be serious about getting one of those.

Jamie Diaz
2016

Jamie Diaz, 15, rolls her eyes as her father inspects a shiny green John Deere® riding lawn mower at Lowe’s. “He can’t be serious about getting one of those. Our yard is like twenty feet wide.” She stands waiting while her father, 53, runs his hand over the yellow seat. He skims the tag. “Wow, look Jamie—with four-wheel power steering I could get around your mother’s flower bed in a cinch,” he says. He circles the mower. He kneels to squeeze one of the wheels. He tries turning the handles. He steps back to admire it in full. “Maybe next year,” he adds wistfully as the two depart toward the check-out aisles with only the two cans of paint they came for.

“

We offer a guilt-free, beautiful lawn.

Anthony Kilmer
2018


Anthony Kilmer, 48, is the owner of Natural Eden Premier Lawn Care®. He prides himself on their unique services. “There are hundreds of studies showing the harms of pesticides on our environment. Our services instead focus on nurturing your lawn to develop naturally thick grass that is healthier in the long run.” The company’s most popular package includes a organic sugar beet fertilizing treatment, a garlic mosquito yard masque, and a 2-step compost-tea-and-plant-oil application. Kilmer adds, “we offer a guilt-free, beautiful lawn for our customers. It is safe for the planet and for their families.”

“

We have to stay competitive.

Rob Paredes
2017

Rob Paredes, 37, started Big Ten Lawns® ten years ago. In the past few years, he’s had a hard time hiring for the work season. “It’s hard work. You’re outside all day. It gets really hot. Not many people here willing to do that, I guess,” Paredes says. He recently filed with the Department of Labor—the first step of many to hire foreign workers in order to fill his open positions. “There’s a lot of paperwork—a lot of back and forth. It’s not that simple to get H-2B visas, but we need the people.” It costs Paredes more to hire foreign labor, but it is still cheaper than raising wages. “We have to stay competitive,” he says.

“

I really would
mow it if I could.

Patty Hirsch
2015


Patty Hirsch, 84, has been looking for help mowing her lawn ever since her neighbor went off to college. “He used to come by every Tuesday,” she says. “I always offered him some money but he wouldn’t take it, or sometimes he’d take a dollar and leave the rest.” Although Hirsch’s lawn has grown far past the length specified by the city code, she’s not too worried. She smiles when she says, “I think they’re taking it easy on me because I’m an old lady. I really would mow it if I could.” Hirsch says she is unsteady on her feet these days. “Do you know anybody who’s looking to make an extra couple bucks?”

“

Who knew you needed grub control and
insect control?

Betty Griffin
1958


Betty Griffin, 29, recommends the magazine Lawn Care to all her neighbors. She claims it has been essential to her lawn care routine. Published by Scotts®, Lawn Care outlines the best practices for tending to the perfect lawn. “It’s so helpful. It recommends great products, too. Who knew you needed grub control and insect control? And it’s region specific so I know I’m getting the best advice for my lawn,” Griffin says. Each spring, sixteen different versions of Lawn Care are sent to millions of homeowners throughout the country, boasting the newest innovations and techniques in lawn care. “I recommend Scotts® ‘family grass seed,’ it worked beautifully for us. I think there’s a coupon in Lawn Care.”

“

The darn stuff took out all my clovers!

Jack Barnes
1954


Jack Barnes, 38, wasn’t happy when he tried a new product from Scotts®: “I used Weed and Feed™ last year and the darn stuff took out all my clovers!” He doesn’t think clovers are harmful—in fact, he argues that they improve his lawn. “They’re helpful little buggers—they’re like fertilizer themselves.” This year, Barnes says he tried something different. “[Scotts®] came out with this thing called Clovex™. Supposed to improve the clovers. I guess they saw where they went wrong killing them all.” Barnes says he applied the new Clovex™ spray last weekend. “Now we wait.”

“

I wonder if that actually works.

Tim Wilson
1951


Tim Wilson, 31, is frustrated by a patch of crabgrass that infiltrated his lawn this spring. “It’s embarrassing! You should see the looks my neighbors give me. They’re worried it’s going to spread.” Wilson claims he pulled out the crabgrass at the first sight of it, but since more has sprouted back. “Everyday, that patch looks a little bigger. You know, I read in Home and Garden something about torching the stuff. I wonder if that actually works.”

“

I'm looking forward to the space.

Richard & Anna Fleming
1952



Richard and Anna Fleming, 29 and 27, are looking for a new home outside the city. They decided to visit the model homes nearby that opened just last weekend. The homes are inexpensive but include all the modern amenities. “We heard about it through a friend, actually. We’re just really looking to get out of the city,” Anna says. “It’s getting a little crowded.” The Flemings decided on the ‘Jubilee’ style, which included an enclosed garage and expandable second floor. “It’ll be perfect for us,” Richard says. “I like the garage. And having a front yard will be nice.” Anna agrees, “I’m looking forward to the space,” she says.

Will Evans
1959



Will Evans, 13, is looking forward to this summer, when he can bring in a little extra money by mowing his neighbor’s lawns. “I mow Mrs. White’s lawn, Mrs. Bennett’s, Mr. Stark’s… sometimes Mr. Mitchell’s, but he likes to do it himself mostly,” Evans says. “Mrs. Bennett’s is the trickiest, I think—she’s got that big flower garden by her door.” Evans says most of his friends like the spend their money on soda, candy, and arcade games, but he claims he is saving most of his money for something special. “I’ve done the math. By the end of July I should be able to buy a bike.”

“

The love of country is inseparably connected with
the love of home.

Andrew J. Downing
1841

Andrew J. Downing, 26, co-author of Treatise on the Theory and Practice of Landscape Gardening, claims that the care for a man’s lawn is important to his standing as a citizen: “The love of country is inseparably connected with the love of home. Whatever, therefore, leads man to assemble the comforts and elegancies of life around his habitation, tends to increase local attachments, and render domestic life more delightful; thus not only augmenting his own enjoyment, but strengthening his patriotism, and making him a better citizen. And there is no employment or recreation which affords the mind greater or more permanent satisfaction, than that of cultivating the earth and adorning our own property.”

“

A lawn can only be cut when the grass is damp.

The Horticulturalist
1841

For those looking to perfect their land, look no further than The Horticulturalist, which outlines the best routine of rolling and mowing: “A good lawn must be mown every ten days or fortnight [...] It is surprising how soon a coarse and wiry bit of sward will become smooth turf, under the magic influences of regular and oft repeated mowing and rolling. Of course, a lawn can only be cut when the grass is damp, and rolling is best performed directly after rain. The English always roll a few hours before using the scythe. On large lawns, a donkey or light horse may be advantageously employed in performing this operation.”
